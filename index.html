ABOUT ME

I started potting in 1999 at Crouch End Art School with Romilly Graham. I make functional art ceramics and enjoy experimenting with many techniques.

A branch of my hobby overload is also being an addicted knitter. The yarn bowls I saw at fairs lacked a knitters touch and understanding. I made five bowls for friends and family, this led to requests from their friends and suggestions that I should sell them. This led to starting The London Yarn Bowl (LYB) in 2014. It has been a friendly venture with ceramic friends Lucky Rutland, Toby Dacre, Mark Brooker and Millie-Beth Wright all contributing their creativity.

The LYB hopes to share creativity and a love of making useful, well made objects with discerning knitters and crocheters - people like us who get pleasure from craft. ​Every LYB is handmade and hand decorated using stoneware, it is twice fired in an electric kiln. Now in its fourth year the LYB has made about 200 bowls. Every bowl is truly unique. I am happy to take on commissions for yarn bowls and other projects, please get in touch to organise this. No project is too small or too big. My smallest commission was a cat mug, my largest was the tableware for a North London restaurant.

My next exhibition is 10 November 2017, please get in touch for an invite.

Happy knitting, Jayne Wright

ART CERAMICS

Works concerned with form and texture.

When I am not making yarn bowls and doing ceramic commissions, I enjoy making ceramics that are concerned with form and texture.

I like my pots to be useful and have a purpose, in keeping with pots of the past. I have spent many hours in museums and galleries in many cities, in many countries, admiring pots by anonymous ceramicists from all over the world, from every age. My forms are inspired by these pots of bygone ages and cultures. I am truly fascinated by the use of pots in just about every culture around the world.

The textures in my work are the textures I see in nature, trees, plants, rocks, the earth and water. I have recently begun to explore colour in ceramics after visiting the geothermal region in New Zealand and being mesmerised by all the colours and oxides that the earth spews from its molten core. The same oxides used in pottery and glazes.

My work is a fusion of landscape, nature and human history. I often develop the same ideas and themes. A recent visit to my favourite museum the V&A has left me wanting to investigate Asian art more, especially India.

"We throw away so many beautiful disposable objects."

NON-DISPOSABLES

In these works I am looking at and questioning what we dispose of and what we reuse, hopefully challenging why we don’t reuse a bit more like we used to.

We throw away so many beautiful disposable objects. Could we make the cups, punnets and plates reusable again? What happened to the milk bottle? Could we bring it back from near extinction?
